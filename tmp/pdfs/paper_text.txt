A B S T R A C T
With the  advent  of  information  explosion,  automatic  multi-document  summarization  has  attracted  widespread  at -
tention from  the  natural  language  pro cessing. Multi-document  summarization  (MDS)  poses  challenges  with  larger  
search and  problem  spaces,  since  lengthy  inputs  often  contain  varying  degrees  of  redundancy  and  contradiction.  
To tackle  these  challenges,  we  propose  a  novel  difficulty-aware  framework  that  enhances  MDS  by  integrating  
curriculum learning  paradigms  into  mixture-of-experts  architectures.  During  the  curriculum  learning  stage,  a  
multi-document difficulty  metric  works  in  tandem  with  the  curriculum  scheduler  to  train  experts  specialized  in  
distinct problem  subspaces.  We  first  employ  the  proposed  multi-document  difficulty  metric  to  partition  the  MDS  
dataset into  subsets  with  distinct  levels  of  difficulty.  Then,  we  adopt  the  difficulty-aware  curriculum  arrangement  
to train  specialized  expert  models  on  different  subsets.  During  the  mixture-of-experts  stage,  we  utilize  a  difficulty -
aware mixture-of-experts  s tructure to  combine  different  models  for  improving  multi-document  summarization.  
Extensive experimental  results  on  two  MDS  datasets  indicate  that  the  proposed  method  achieves  state-of-the-art  
performance among  length-constrained  methods  and  delivers  competitive  results  compared  to  other  strong  base -
lines with  greater  parameters . These  results  demonstrate  the  effectiveness  of  combining  curriculum  learning  and  
mixture-of-experts, implying  a  promising  direction  for  multi-document  summarization.
1. Introduction
With the  exponential  growth  of  textual  information  on  the  Internet,  
it is  becoming  increasingly  impossible  for  people  to  read  and  compre -
hend all  of  it  in  a  short  time,  exacerbating  the  problem  of  information  
overload [ 1]. In  the  context  of  information  explosion,  automatic  multi -
document summarization  technology  arose  as  needed.  Specifically,
it helps  individuals  find  the  information  they  need  more  efficiently  from  
enormous amounts  of  text  by  providing  a  succinct  summary  of  multiple  
related documents.  Due  to  its  wide  range  of  application  scenarios,  au -
tomatic multi-document  summarization  has  drawn  significant  attention  
from researchers  in  recent  years,  including  scientific  summarization  [ 2], 
Wikipedia generation  [ 3], and  medical  abstraction  [ 4].

Multi-document summarization  (MDS)  aims  to  create  brief  sum -
maries that  contain  essential  and  unique  details  from  a  collection  of  
topic-related documents  [ 5]. Typically,  MDS  can  be  divided  into  two  
types: abstractive  MDS  and  extractive  MDS.  More  specifically,  extrac -
tive MDS  aims  to  extract  the  best  phrases  or  sentences  from  source  
documents to  cover  salient  information,  whereas  abstractive  MDS  fa -
cilitates the  creation  of  summaries  by  paraphrasing  the  main  ideas  of  
source materials.  With  the  advent  of  large-scale  datasets  and  the  evo -
lution of  powerful  pre-trained  language  models  (PLMs),  there  has  been  
substantial progress  in  abstractive  MDS  in  recent  years  [ 6]. Since  the  
paradigm of  efficient  fine-tuning  based  on  pre-trained  language  models  
has become  the  dominant  style  [ 7], our  work  falls  into  abstractive  MDS.
Unlike single-document  summarization  (SDS)  which  simply  sum -
marizes the  main  content  in  one  document,  MDS  is  inherently  more  
complex [ 8]. We  observe  the  following  two  main  challenges:  (1)  Larger  
search space.  Typically,  MDS  has  longer  input  but  fewer  training  sam -
ples, resulting  in  a  larger  search  space  [ 9]. For  instance,  the  popular  SDS  
dataset CNN/DailyMail  [ 10] has  287  K  training  samples  with  an  average  
of 687  words,  while  the  benchmark  MDS  dataset  Multi-News  [ 11] has  
45 K  training  samples  with  an  average  of  1789  words.  (2)  Larger  prob -
lem space.  Obviously,  collections  of  documents  from  multiple  sources,  
times, and  perspectives  exhibit  varying  degrees  of  redundancy  and  con -
tradiction. Due  to  the  complexity  of  multi-document  inputs  varies,  MDS  
has a  larger  problem  space  [ 12].
Recent years  have  seen  a  large  number  of  dedicated  attempts  to  over -
come the  aforementioned  MDS  issues  [ 13]. For  larger  search  spaces,  
previous studies  have  either  extended  the  maximum  encoding  length  
by refining  the  standard  attention  mechanism  of  Transformer  [ 14] or  
extracted high-quality  meta-documents  from  raw  input  using  an  extract -
then-summarize pipeline  [ 15,16]. However,  the  former  technique  often  
bears greater  computational  and  time  overhead,  whereas  the  latter  
method is  hampered  by  inescapable  cascading  losses.  Notably,  these  
methods are  confined  to  lengthy  multi-document  inputs  but  neglect  ef -
ficient training  using  fewer  instances.  For  larger  problem  spaces,  prior  
work [ 17] has  attempted  to  discard  easier  and  harder  examples  to  help  
the model  converge  quickly.  Obviously,  this  arbitrary  approach  may  
induce model  degradation  due  to  its  lower  generalization  capabilities.
Recent studies  [ 18,19] have  shown  that  curriculum  learning  plays  a  
positive role  in  improving  model  training  efficiency  and  generalization  
ability by  gradually  introducing  more  challenging  samples.  Since  MDS  
involves processing  multi-document  inputs  with  varying  complexity,  it  
makes sense  to  adopt  a  curriculum  learning  paradigm  to  train  the  model  
in a  larger  search  space.  In  addition,  the  use  of  a  mixture-of-experts  
structure to  assign  various  experts  to  handle  distinct  subtasks  has  proven  
to be  beneficial  for  complex  tasks  [ 20]. For  the  difficult  MDS  task,  it  
is reasonable  to  utilize  a  mixture-of-experts  architecture  to  incorporate  
multiple models  working  on  specific  types  of  data.
To deal  with  the  larger  search  and  problem  spaces,  we  propose  
a novel  CLMoE  framework  for  improving  MDS  that  incorporates  a  
Curriculum Learning  paradigm  into  a  Mixture-of-Experts  architecture.  
As shown  in  Fig. 1 , the corresponding  experts  are  first  trained  us -
ing curriculum  learning,  and  then  these  experts  are  aggregated  in  a  
difficulty-aware mixture-of-experts  architecture.  To  train  experts  spe -
cialized in  various  problem  subspaces,  the  multi-document  difficulty  
metrics and  the  difficulty-aware  curriculum  learning  work  in  tandem.  
Specifically, we  employ  a  difficulty  metric  designed  for  the  multi -
document inputs  to  partition  the  original  dataset  into  three  subsets  with  
distinct difficulties:  subset  #Easy,  subset  #Normal,  and  subset  #Hard.  
Then, we  use  the  proposed  difficulty-aware  curriculum  arrangement  to  
train corresponding  experts  on  subsets  of  different  difficulties,  including  
expert #Easy,  expert  #Normal,  and  expert  #Hard.  Lastly,  we  propose  a  
difficulty-aware mixture-of-experts  structure  for  aggregating  all  experts,  
which consists  of  a  gating  network  and  a  summary  selector.
We assess  the  proposed  CLMoE  framework  implemented  with  BART  
on two  MDS  benchmark  datasets:  Multi-News  and  DUC-2004.  Automatic  
evaluation results  using  the  widely  used  Recall-Oriented  Understudy  for  
Gisting Evaluation  (ROUGE)  show  that  the  proposed  approach  outper -
forms input-constrained  methods  and  achieves  comparable  performance  
to several  input-unrestricted  baselines.  In  particular,  we  thoroughly  ex -
amine the  effectiveness  of  different  difficulty  metrics  and  curriculum  
schedules. Lastly,  we  carry  out  detailed  ablation  studies  to  further  vali -
date the  efficacy  of  removable  components,  such  as  the  curriculum  learn -
ing paradigm  and  the  mixture-of-experts  architecture.  These  encourag -
ing results  demonstrate  the  efficacy  of  incorporating  the  Curriculum

2. Related  work
This section  offers  a  brief  overview  of  related  work  closely  aligned  
with the  proposed  CLMoE,  including  curriculum  learning  and  mixture -
of-experts.
2.1. Curriculum  learning
Curriculum learning  (CL)  is  a  typical  training  paradigm  first  proposed  
by Bengio  et  al.  [ 21] in  2009.  It  involves  efficiently  training  a  model  on  
a succession  of  examples  in  a  carefully  planned  manner,  which  includes  
determining the  difficulty  of  the  examples  and  arranging  the  learning  
process [ 22]. The  way  humans  learn  by  first  understanding  basic  con -
cepts and  then  moving  on  to  more  advanced  ones  serves  as  inspiration  
for this.
In the  context  of  large-scale  datasets  and  large-parameter  models,  
CL seeks  to  train  the  model  more  efficiently  by  gradually  increasing  the  
complexity of  samples,  which  has  been  proven  to  accelerate  model  train -
ing and  achieve  better  local  optima  states  [ 23]. As  CL  garners  increasing  
interest from  a  growing  number  of  researchers,  improved  algorithms  
utilizing CL  have  been  advancing  in  recent  years  [ 19]. In  particular,  
it has  found  extensive  application  across  various  natural  language  pro -
cessing (NLP)  domains,  such  as  reading  comprehension  [ 24], machine  
translation [ 25], and  question  answering  [ 26], demonstrating  superior  
performance and  faster  convergence  speeds.  However,  there  have  been  
only a  few  attempts  to  use  curriculum  learning  methods  for  automatic  
text summarization.  Specifically,  Kano  et  al.  [ 27] propose  an  efficient  
curriculum learning  method  for  training  summarization  models  from  
noisy data.  To  effectively  summarize  mental  health  posts,  Sotudeh  et  al.  
[28] incorporate  a  confidence-aware  curriculum  learning  approach  into  
the summarization  framework.  In  addition,  Sun  et  al.  [ 17] consider  a  
data selection  curriculum  for  abstractive  text  summarization  that  in -
cludes a  margin-aware  list-wise  ranking  loss.  It  is  worth  noting  that  these  
beneficial attempts  are  limited  to  the  SDS  task.
To our  knowledge,  curriculum  learning  is  still  in  its  infancy  
and has  not  been  explored  for  the  MDS  task.  Since  multi-document  
summarization involves  processing  documents  with  varying  degrees  of
redundancy and  contradiction,  the  data  complexity  fluctuates  greatly,  
making it  ideal  for  curriculum  learning.  Inspired  by  the  excellent  work  
of Hacohen  and  Weinshall  [ 29], we  apply  the  CL  paradigm  to  the  
multi-document summarization  task  for  the  first  time.  Recently,  Kong  
et al.  [ 30] introduced  an  adaptive  CL  framework  that  dynamically  
adapts the  difficulty  score  to  the  current  state  of  model  during  training.  
Similarly, SPrint  [ 31] proposed  using  adaptive  curriculum  and  mem -
ory replay  to  achieve  self-paced  continuous  learning.  Unlike  previous  
studies that  emphasize  dynamic  adaptation  in  general  curriculum  learn -
ing, our  proposed  curriculum  learning  and  difficulty  scores  are  derived  
from domain-specific  characteristics  directly  related  to  the  MDS  task.  
Specifically, we  propose  task-specific  heuristic  difficulty  metrics  to  ad -
dress the  unique  challenges  of  MDS,  including  contextual  coherence,  
topic similarity,  and  lexical  analysis.  It  is  worth  emphasizing  that  the  
proposed approach  is  not  only  intended  to  enhance  curriculum  learn -
ing itself,  but  also  to  explore  the  integration  of  CL  paradigm  with  
mixture-of-experts architecture.
2.2. Mixture-of-experts
Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)  is  a  classic  neural  network  in  the  field  of  
machine learning,  first  proposed  by  Jacobs  et  al.  [ 32] in  1991.  Since  it  is  
difficult for  a  model  to  be  proficient  in  all  areas,  more  complex  jobs  can  
be done  by  combining  experts  with  diverse  skills.  Inspired  by  this,  MoE  
first divides  the  problem  space  into  several  subspaces,  and  then  brings  
together expert  models  that  specialize  in  each  subspace  to  form  a  more  
powerful and  flexible  model  [ 33]. More  precisely,  it  employs  a  gating  
network to  select  appropriate  experts  to  process  the  input  data,  and  then  
aggregates their  predictions  into  a  final  output.
As of  now,  the  MoE  architecture  has  garnered  widespread  atten -
tion from  the  machine  learning  community  [ 20]. For  instance,  Eigen  
et al.  [ 34] present  a  deep  MoE  model  made  up  of  multi-layer  routers  
and experts  to  improve  the  capacity  of  processing  complicated  data.  
Furthermore, Shazeer  et  al.  [ 35] propose  to  increase  training  stability  
and reduce  computing  costs  with  a  sparse  MoE,  which  simplifies  the  
MoE structure  by  sparse  the  gating  network  output  for  each  sample.  
Recently, Zhao  et  al.  [ 36] propose  a  hierarchical  MoE  as  an  extension,  
which handles  tasks  by  organizing  experts  in  a  tree-like  structure.
Although MoE  architectures  have  proven  successful  in  a  variety  
of NLP  scenarios,  including  recommendation  [ 37], machine  transla -
tion [ 38], and  language  modeling  [ 39], they  have  not  yet  been  seri -
ously considered  for  automatic  text  summarization.  Specifically,  Goyal  
et al.  [ 40] propose  a  novel  architecture  involving  multiple  decoders  
in a  mixture-of-experts  structure  to  obtain  more  flexible  summaries.  
Moreover, Ravaut  et  al.  [ 41] introduce  a  multi-task  re-ranking  frame -
work based  on  the  MoE  architecture  to  address  the  exposure  bias  of  
abstractive summarization.  However,  these  studies  mentioned  above  are  
limited to  single-document  summarization.
To our  knowledge,  there  is  no  related  work  utilizing  the  MoE  archi -
tecture to  improve  multi-document  summarization.  As  we  mentioned  
earlier, multi-document  summarization  entails  condensing  salient  con -
tent across  collections  of  documents  with  varying  degrees  of  redundancy  
and contradiction.  Due  to  the  diversified  multi-document  inputs,  the  
problem space  can  be  partitioned  into  several  subspaces  according  to  
data complexity,  which  is  ideal  for  the  MoE  architecture.  Unlike  previ -
ous work,  we  integrate  the  CL  paradigm  into  the  MoE  architecture  as  the  
CLMoE framework  to  improve  multi-document  summarization.  In  par -
ticular, this  is  the  first  attempt  to  apply  the  MoE  architecture  to  the  MDS  
task. In  this  paper,  we  effectively  integrate  several  experts  with  vary -
ing levels  of  difficulty  to  handle  the  problem  space  of  multi-complexity  
in multi-document  summarization.  A  detailed  overview  of  MoE  can  be  
found in  Fedus  et  al.  [ 42].

3.2. Curriculum  learning  basics
Inspired by  the  meaningful  order  of  human  learning,  Bengio  et  al.  
[21] introduce  the  training  paradigm  of  CL  to  sample  training  data  from  
easy to  hard.  As  shown  in  Fig. 2 , the process  of  curriculum  learning  is  to  
first sort  training  instances  according  to  a  pre-defined  difficulty  metric,  
and then  take  different  curriculum  arrangements  to  help  model  training.  
In this  way,  not  only  the  performance  and  convergence  speed  are  im -
proved, but  also  the  generalization  capacity  is  increased.  As  we  discussed  
earlier, it  is  critical  for  curriculum  learning  to  train  the  model  by  effi -
ciently arranging  training  examples  from  simple  to  complex.  Following  
Kano et  al.  [ 27], we  introduce  four  common  curriculum  arrangements  
for curriculum  learning,  including  OnePass,  BabySteps,  Random,  and  
Reverse curriculum.  The  overview  of  th ese curriculum  arrangements  is  
shown in  Fig. 2 .

3.3. Mixture-of-experts  basics
Mixture-of-Experts consists  of  multiple  separated  neural  networks  
(Experts) and  a  gating  network,  which  can  be  traced  back  to  ensemble  
learning. As  illustrated  in  Fig. 3 , MoE first  separates  the  input  dataset  
into several  task-relevant  subsets,  and  then  assigns  distinct  subsets  to  
different experts  for  training.  Since  each  expert  specializes  in  processing  
a subset  of  the  entire  dataset,  it  achieves  better  performance  than  others.  
Further, the  gating  network  is  designed  to  effectively  select  appropriate  
experts for  different  inputs.
As one  of  the  core  components  of  MoE,  the  gating  network  plays  an  
important role  in  effectively  utilizing  the  capabilities  of  various  experts.  
Specifically, it  assigns  each  piece  of  input  data  a  weight  that  indicates  
the various  contributions  made  by  experts  in  processing  the  data.  As  
another key  component  of  MoE,  the  Experts,  a  group  of  neural  net -
works specialized  in  different  subsets,  play  a  crucial  role  in  improving  
the overall  performance.  During  the  training  phase,  the  input  data  is  as -
signed to  different  experts  for  training  by  the  gating  network.  During  
the inference  phase,  experts  selected  by  the  gating  network  produce  
corresponding predictions  for  the  inputs.  Lastly,  these  predictions  are  
combined with  weights  assigned  by  the  gating  network  to  form  the  final  
output.

4.2. Difficulty-aware  curriculum  learning
As an  adaptive  MDS  framework,  difficulty-aware  curriculum  learn -
ing mainly  consists  of  two  stages:  (1)  quantitative  difficulty  estimation  
through multi-document  difficulty  metric,  and  (2)  dynamic  curricu -
lum scheduling  using  difficulty-aware  curriculum  arrangement.  This  
two-stage mechanism  enables  models  to  develop  robust  feature  repre -
sentations by  allocating  computational  resources  proportionally  to  the  
empirically measured  complexity  of  training  instances.
Quantitative difficulty  estimation.  We  first  sort  the  training  ex -
amples in  ascending  order  according  to  the  multi-document  difficulty  
metrics discussed  above.  Instead  of  discarding  data  that  is  too  easy  or  
too difficult,  we  group  training  examples  according  to  the  final  difficulty  
scores, including  subset  #Easy,  subset  #Normal,  and  subset  #Hard.  As  
shown in  Fig. 5 , we split  the  training  dataset  with  Gaussian  distribution  
into three  subsets  at  a  ratio  of  1:3:1.  A  preliminary  analysis  reveals  that  
there is  considerable  variability  in  difficulty  scores  across  examples.  If  
the model  encounters  too  many  examples  with  substantial  difficulty  dif -
ferences or  too  complex  instances  in  the  early  stage  of  training,  it  may  
experience information  overload  or  overfitting.  It  is  crucial  to  gradually  
process examples  of  similar  difficulty  through  group  training,  as  it  can  
not only  improve  the  efficiency  of  model  training,  but  also  reduce  the  
risk of  overfitting.
Dynamic curriculum  schedule.  In  this  part,  we  introduce  the  
difficulty-aware curriculum  arrangement  to  train  corresponding  expert  
models for  different  subsets.  Specifically,  we  employ  distinct  train -
ing strategies  to  accommodate  data  of  varying  complexity,  which  is  
illustrated in  Algorithm 1 .
For the  subset  #Easy,  we  employ  reverse  curriculum  training  to  ob -
tain expert  #Easy.  By  training  with  more  difficult  examples  initially,  the  
model can  learn  key  features  more  quickly.  It  is  noteworthy  that  the  re -
verse curriculum  only  works  for  the  subset  with  simple  instances.  One  
possible explanation  is  that  relatively  hard  examples  in  the  subset  #Easy  
are acceptable  for  the  initial  model.  For  the  subset  #Normal,  we  adopt  
a progressive  training  strategy  to  obtain  expert  #Normal.  Specifically,  
the model  starts  by  processing  relatively  simple  examples,  and  then  
gradually introduces  more  complex  ones.  Such  training  paradigm  not  
only enhances  model  robustness  through  gradual  confidence  building,  
but also  significantly  improves  its  generalization  capability  on  challeng -
ing examples.  In  particular,  we  implement  progressive  training  using  
the widely  used  BabySteps  curriculum  in  the  following  experiments.  
Inspired by  Xia  et  al.  [ 49], we  train  the  corresponding  expert  in  a  ran -
dom manner  for  the  subset  #Hard.  Since  neither  progressive  training  
nor reverse  training  provides  any  benefit  for  high-difficulty  scenarios,  
we decide  to  use  a  randomized  training  approach.  This  will  be  discussed  
in detail  in  Section 6.4 .

4.3. Difficulty-aware  mixture-of-experts
Once difficulty-aware  experts  have  been  trained  using  the  proposed  
difficulty-aware curriculum  learning,  it  is  necessary  to  unite  them  to  
maintain generalization  performance  under  different  difficulty  levels.  
As illustrated  in  Fig. 6 , the difficulty-aware  gating  network  and  the  
summary selector  work  together  to  form  the  difficulty-aware  mixture -
of-experts.
Difficulty-aware gating  network.  As  we  mentioned  in  Section 3.3 , 
the gating  network  plays  an  important  role  in  the  mixture  of  difficulty -
aware experts.  It  is  an  essential  mechanism  in  the  mixture-of-experts  that  
responds flexibly  to  complex  tasks  by  regulating  the  flow  of  information  
between different  components.  Since  multi-document  summarization  
aims to  achieve  a  balance  between  efficiency  and  performance,  we  adopt  
a heuristic  gating  strategy.  We  fully  acknowledge  that  it  inherently  lacks  
the adaptability  of  learnable  gating  networks  for  capturing  complex,  
data-driven patterns.  In  contrast,  a  learnable  gating  mechanism  enables  
data-driven optimization  of  the  input  routing  path  to  specialized  experts.  
This data-driven  approach  has  the  potential  for  superior  performance

plemented and  IDF  weighting  disabled.  All  reported  BERTScore  values

5.3. Implementation  details
Pre-processing. As  the  core  of  CLMoE  framework  for  multi-document  
summarization, we  construct  three  subsets  of  varying  difficulties  from  
the original  Multi-News  dataset  using  the  proposed  multi-document  
difficulty metric.  In  particular,  we  remove  erroneous  article-summary  
pairs, which  are  noisy  data  caused  by  failures  to  obtain  the  source  ar -
ticles. For  noisy  data,  the  total  length  of  source  documents  is  less  than  
that of  the  reference  summary,  making  it  difficult  for  the  summarizer  
to generate  high-quality  summaries.  Since  all  the  difficulty  heuristics  
contribute to  the  proposed  multi-document  difficulty  score,  we  adopt  a  
weighted summation  with  equal  hyperparameter  values  to  calculate  the  
final difficulty  score.  To  effectively  partition  the  dataset  by  difficulty  
scores while  ensuring  that  the  subset  are  adequate  for  training,  we  split  
the original  Multi-News  dataset  into  3  subsets  with  a  ratio  of  1:3:1,  in -
cluding subset  #Easy,  subset  #Normal,  and  subset  #Hard.  Afterward,  
the Score  
Easy  
is set  to  the  maximum  difficulty  score  in  the  subset  #Easy,  
while the  hyperparameter  Score  
Hard  
is set  to  the  minimum  difficulty  
score in  the  subset  #Hard.
Semantic difficulty  scoring.  As  discussed  in  previous  section,  the  
implementation of  semantic  difficulty  scoring  consists  of  two  key  
stages: embedding  extraction  and  similarity  computation.  To  obtain  
high-quality contextualized  semantic  representations  of  input  texts,  the  
popular BART  (Bidirectional  and  Auto-Regressive  Transformers)  model  
is adopted  as  the  semantic  encoder.  In  our  experiments,  we  employ  
the BART-large-cnn  model  from  Hugging  Face  library  as  the  embed -
ding model.  For  a  given  text  input,  we  extract  the  embeddings  from  
the final  hidden  layer  of  the  semantic  encoder.  More  specifically,  we  
utilize the  hidden  state  of  the  [CLS]  token  from  the  final  layer  of  BART -
large-cnn, which  serves  as  a  comprehensive  representation  of  the  entire  
input sequence.  This  process  converts  the  input  text  into  a  dense  fixed -
dimensional vector  that  effectively  captures  its  semantic  essence.  Once  
the semantic  embeddings  for  a  pair  of  texts  are  obtained,  we  quantify  
their semantic  relationship  by  calculating  the  cosine  similarity  between  
their respective  vector  representations.  The  co sine similarity  metric  
effectively captures  the  directional  alignment  in  the  semantic  space,  pro -
viding a  reliable  indicator  of  their  spatial  proximity  as  well  as  semantic  
consistency. It  is  important  to  note  that  the  semantic  difficulty  scores  
serve solely  to  establish  a  relative  difficulty  hierarchy  across  documents.  
In this  way,  lower  inter-document  semantic  similarity  is  interpreted  
as indicating  greater  difficulty.  Consequently,  we  can  create  stratified  
training sets  without  implying  precise  difficulty  scores.
Backbone model.  Following  Pasunuru  et  al.  [ 52], we  select  BART  
as the  backbone  model  to  implement  the  CLMoE  framework  for  multi -
document summarization.  Distinct  from  conventional  approaches  that  
rely solely  on  bidirectional  architectures  (e.g.,  BERT)  or  purely  autore -
gressive frameworks  (e.g.,  GPT-2),  BART  synergistically  merges  a  bidi -
rectional encoder  with  a  left-to-right  autoregressive  decoder  designed  
for linguistically  coherent  generation.  Both  the  encoder  and  decoder  
are built  upon  multiple  layers  of  self-attention  mechanisms  and  feed -
forward networks,  augmented  with  layer  normalization  and  residual  
connections to  ensure  stable  training  and  efficient  gradient  propagation.  
Additionally, while  PEGASUS  is  specifically  designed  for  summariza -
tion, our  analysis  reveals  that  its  single-document-oriented  pre-training  
paradigm is  less  adaptable  to  multi-document  summarization.  Due  to  its  
unique neural  architecture  that  effectively  integrates  bidirectional  en -
coding and  autoregressive  decoding  mechanisms,  BART  demonstrates  
exceptional suitability  for  MDS  tasks.  Specifically,  BART  can  not  only  
model contexts  across  multiple  documents,  but  also  generate  coherent  
summaries through  its  autoregressive  decoder.  It  is  important  to  empha -
size that  our  CLMoE  framework  is  inherently  model-agnostic  and  can  be  
flexibly integrated  with  various  backbone  models.
Hyperparameter settings.  In  our  experiments,  we  implement  the  
large configuration  of  BART  on  the  top  of  fairseq  library,  which  includes  
12 encoder–decoder  layers,  16  attention  heads,  and  1024  embedding
dimensions. Specifically,  we  train  difficulty-aware  experts  by  effec -
tively fine-tuning  the  BART-large-cnn  checkpoint  on  different  subsets.  
Following the  common  BART  fine-tuning  procedure,  we  optimize  the  
cross entropy  loss  function  using  the  Adam  optimizer.  More  precisely,  a  
dropout rate  of  0.1,  an  initial  learning  rate  of  2 e

Methods with  greater  input  lengths.  Typically,  the  maximum  encod -

methods with  greater  input  lengths  can  achieve  better  performance  at

From this  table,  we  can  readily  draw  the  following  conclusions.  
Compared with  BART  (“w/o  CLMoE”),  MDS-CL  (“w/o  MoE”)  shows  
significant gains  in  terms  of  ROUGE-1/2/L,  indicating  that  the  cur -
riculum learning  technique  is  a  promising  approach  for  the  MDS  task.  
Additionally, the  average  performance  of  BART-CLMoE  is  much  superior  
to BART  (“w/o  CLMoE”)  and  MDS-CL  (“w/o  MoE”).  More  specifically,  
BART-CLMoE exhibits  an  average  enhancement  of  0.52  compared  to  
MDS-CL, demonstrating  the  advantageous  impact  of  the  MoE  archi -
tecture on  improving  multi-document  summarization.  These  positive  
results demonstrate  that  all  removable  components  play  an  integral  role  
in the  CLMoE  framework  for  boosting  multi-document  summarization.
7. Conclusion  and  future  work
In this  paper,  we  propose  a  novel  CLMoE  framework  to -
wards Curriculum  Learning  of  multi-document  summarization  utilizing  
difficulty-aware Mixture-of-Experts.  Specifically,  we  first  train  difficulty -
aware experts  on  subsets  of  varying  difficulties  using  a  curriculum  
learning paradigm,  and  then  aggregate  these  experts  specialized  in  
various problem  subspaces  using  a  mixture-of-experts  architecture.  To  
achieve this,  the  proposed  CLMoE  consists  of  three  main  components:  
multi-document difficulty  metric,  difficulty-aware  curriculum  learning,  
and difficulty-aware  mixture-of-experts.  More  specifically,  we  propose  
a multi-document  difficulty  metric  composed  of  semantic  analysis  and  
lexical analysis  to  construct  three  subsets  with  various  difficulties  from  
the original  dataset:  subset  #Easy,  subset  #Normal,  and  subset  #Hard.  
Furthermore, we  present  a  difficulty-aware  curriculum  learning  that  uses  
distinct curriculum  arrangements  for  different  subsets  to  train  corre -
sponding experts,  including  expert  #Easy,  expert  #Normal,  and  expert  
#Hard. Once  experts  are  obtained,  we  adopt  a  difficulty-aware  mixture -
of-experts structure  to  integrate  them  to  improve  multi-document  
summarization. Extensive  evaluation  results  on  two  MDS  benchmark  
datasets demonstrate  that  our  proposed  BART-CLMoE  achieves  SOTA  
performance compared  to  methods  with  limited  input  lengths,  and  
achieves comparable  results  to  other  methods  with  greater  input  lengths.
In future  work,  we  will  further  investigate  more  effective  difficulty  
metrics for  multi-document  summarization  tasks.  In  addition,  we  will  
explore the  utilization  of  a  hybrid  methodology  integrating  progressive  
training and  reverse  training  to  facilitate  difficulty-aware  curriculum  
learning. Moreover,  we  will  introduce  a  simple  learnable  component  
to improve  the  heuristic  gating  network,  achieving  a  trade-off  between  
heuristic and  learnable  gating.  In  particular,  it  is  worthwhile  to  apply  
the proposed  CLMoE  to  other  challenging  scenarios.
